Penguins

Penguins are a group of aquatic flightless birds, which live mainly in the Souther Hemisphere, with one species as the exception, which lives in the Northern Hemisphere. They spend equal amounts of time on land and in the sea, which is why they are well adapted for life in the ocean water.

Habitat

Even though most penguings are native to the Southern Hemisphere, they are not onlfy found in cold climates, but also in temperate zones in some cases, such as the Galápagos penguin. Nevertheless, although the Arctic and Antartic climates are similar, no penguins can be found in the Arctic.

Anatomy

They are highly adapted to aquatic life, having wings that serve them as flippers for swimming, becoming unuseful for flying. Both floatage and insulation from cold waters are possible because of the layer of air located within their black and white plumage, which is also useful for camouflage.

Feeding

The majority of penguins feed on various forms of sea life such as squid, fish or krill, which they are able to cath with their bills, and are able to swallow while swimming. Their spiny tongues and powerful jaws allow them to be able to catch slippery prey as well.

Breeding

Almost every species of penguins breed in large colonies, apart from the Fiordland and yellow-eyed species. Depending on the species, the amount of members that integrate these colonies varies from a couple hundreds to several hundred thousand in some cases.

Most penguins lay two eggs in a clutch, but the king and emperor penguins, which are some of the largest species lay only one. The incubation duties are shared by all penguins, except for the emperor species, in which the male does it all.

Conservation status

Most of the living penguin species have declining population, according to the last assessment made by the IUCN Red List, which was made in 2018 for every species except for the Erect-crested species, which had its last assessment made in 2016. The conservation status of the different species ranges from Least Concern to Endagnered. Listed below are the most Endangered and Near Threatened species.

Galápagos penguin

Incubation

Even though the rate of the same pair of penguins recoupling varies widely, they do form monogamous pairs for the breeding season. The incubation shifts can last days or weeks because of their need of feeding in the sea.
